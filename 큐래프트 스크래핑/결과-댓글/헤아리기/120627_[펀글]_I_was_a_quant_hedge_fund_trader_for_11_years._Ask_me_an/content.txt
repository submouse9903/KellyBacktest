I can't reveal details of trading algorithms/strategies, but I'll do my best to give candid answers about anything quant related.U16DDid you build your own software for quant analysis? What goes into the design of such software?Yes, we did develop all of our own in-house software for quantitative analysis and trading, everything from simulation & backtesting, to data warehousing, to actually executing live trades in the market. There were definitely a few software libraries and some open source stuff that we relied on, but everything else was custom built in-house. The one off-the-shelf program I can ever remember paying for was MATLAB, and that lasted only for about a year. There are a ton of decisions that go into the design of automated trading systems, but a few major themes come to mind: - Safety. When you're writing a program that makes real-money trades on its own, you want to be pretty darn sure that it's not going to go haywire and make the wrong trades. I spent endless hours on code safety. Of course you can never achieve 100% safety, and creating quant programs is not quite the same as writing control code for the space shuttle where human lives are on the line, but still, the monetary stakes tend to be pretty high in pro trading and losing money to a simple bug in your code is very demoralizing, not to mention potentially fatal for your career. - Overfitting (Avoiding It). It's diabolically easy to overfit or overtrain a trading model so that the model looks super profitable on in-sample/historical data. Then you start trading it live (out of sample) and it tanks. This is Data Mining. Avoiding it is probably the core challenge in quant trading. It's harder than it sounds because one can overfit a model without even really knowing it or being aware that you overfit. By the time it becomes obvious, it's too late, you already took the P&L hit. So, we developed all sorts of techniques for mitigating the risk of overfitting--some of them code techniques, and others more process-oriented things. - Parsimony. This ties into the two points above. Once you start trading live markets, one of the counterintuitive things you learn is that simple models generally perform best. Parsimonious models are more robust to crazy data inputs; they're also easier to analyze if they lose money. The idea of a robotics PhD coming in and building a fancy neural network tradebot or whatever is a myth. Complexity kills. Lastly, as a general principle, I'm not a believer in using high-tech GUIs or frameworks in quant software.
