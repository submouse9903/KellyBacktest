I was a quant hedge fund trader for 11 years. Ask me anything.


I spent 3 years as a junior quant at Goldman Sachs and 8 years running my own statistical arbitrage portfolio at a major New York hedge fund. I've ridden the rollercoaster from start to finish: put on my first trade the week before 9/11, endured the 2007 quant meltdown, and at the peak of my career I was trading more than 100m shares a month. By 2010 I had left the industry and now I'm in Silicon Valley doing tech startups. I can't reveal details of trading algorithms/strategies, but I'll do my best to give candid answers about anything quant related.

Did you build your own software for quant analysis? What goes into the design of such software?
Yes, we did develop all of our own in-house software for quantitative analysis and trading, everything from simulation & backtesting, to data warehousing, to actually executing live trades in the market. 

There were definitely a few software libraries and some open source stuff that we relied on, but everything else was custom built in-house. The one off-the-shelf program I can ever remember paying for was MATLAB, and that lasted only for about a year. 

There are a ton of decisions that go into the design of automated trading systems, but a few major themes come to mind: 

- Safety. When you're writing a program that makes real-money trades on its own, you want to be pretty darn sure that it's not going to go haywire and make the wrong trades. I spent endless hours on code safety. Of course you can never achieve 100% safety, and creating quant programs is not quite the same as writing control code for the space shuttle where human lives are on the line, but still, the monetary stakes tend to be pretty high in pro trading and losing money to a simple bug in your code is very demoralizing, not to mention potentially fatal for your career. 

- Overfitting (Avoiding It). It's diabolically easy to overfit or overtrain a trading model so that the model looks super profitable on in-sample/historical data. Then you start trading it live (out of sample) and it tanks. This is Data Mining. Avoiding it is probably the core challenge in quant trading. It's harder than it sounds because one can overfit a model without even really knowing it or being aware that you overfit. By the time it becomes obvious, it's too late, you already took the P&L hit. So, we developed all sorts of techniques for mitigating the risk of overfitting--some of them code techniques, and others more process-oriented things. 

- Parsimony. This ties into the two points above. Once you start trading live markets, one of the counterintuitive things you learn is that simple models generally perform best. Parsimonious models are more robust to crazy data inputs; they're also easier to analyze if they lose money. The idea of a robotics PhD coming in and building a fancy neural network tradebot or whatever is a myth. Complexity kills. 

Lastly, as a general principle, I'm not a believer in using high-tech GUIs or frameworks in quant software. The programs I wrote were mostly down and dirty UNIX-style stuff. :)

In general, do quants provide a useful service to humanity (by setting price signals, etc)?
Quant fund managers contribute a useful service to the economy. They set price signals and provide needed liquidity to the market. Same rationale as any fund manager, quant or otherwise. 

Whether or not that is a useful service to humanity as a whole, I don't know. That's a pretty high standard. It requires deep consideration, and I also think it's pretty dependent on your own philosophy of life, etc. 

My personal view is that any profession which improves the functioning of our economy, and does so in an honest way, no matter how indirect or minor, is honorable. 

(Quant models, on the other hand, are a more subtle story. The controversy over malfunctioning quant models is the finance world's equivalent of the gun control debate.)

Explain some abandoned / public trading algorithm and how it works.
Pairs trading. It used to be a consistent money maker, but now that the general strategy has been written up in academic articles and numerous books, the juice has largely been squeezed out. (As always, there are exceptions.) 

Basic pairs trading involves going long one stock X and short a similar/correlated stock Y, and thus trading the spread X-Y. The problem is then reduced to modeling the spread, whether it's mean reverting or whatever.

What level of math was required to do your job?
Quant trading is all statistics. You probably only need an undergraduate-level understanding of stats to do the job, but the traders who really knew statistics like the back of their hand had an advantage because it meant they could interpret results more accurately and spend less time chasing down dead ends. 

Optimization theory can also be pretty important if you use optimizers in your trading. 

Algorithm design and complexity theory definitely come into play when you are implementing strategies and writing code. 

That's about it. PhD-level math, never. Even basic calculus rarely got used. I don't recall ever integrating anything.

Was it necessary to continually come up with new edges/algorithms to maintain profitability?
Absolutely, no question. Profitable quantitative strategies tend to have a shelf life, so continually generating new strategies and algorithms is just about the only way to maintain an edge over the long term. 

There are exceptions, where a single strategy can be milked for years on end, but that's rare. 

Also, this is less true for HFT, where the edge is more technology-driven.

What single law change would maximize BOTH driving $ out of trading & into SMBs / startups?
It would be easy for lawmakers to drive people out of quantitative trading--just levy a per-share tax on each trade. A tax of even 1 penny per share would effectively shut down the entire HFT industry and most of the quant ecosystem, for equities, at least. (Note: I'm definitely not in favor of this. Just speaking hypothetically.) 

I can't think of any single law that would accomplish the dual goal of driving people out of trading and into startups.

What language do you use to code? How proficient do you need to be in coding to do your job?
C++, Python, Perl, Unix shell scripts.

If ones goal to work for a hedge fund, whats better? S&T BB or Prop Trader at a Small Shop?
In sales & trading, you'd likely gain a broad understanding of the market and the various players in it. 

In prop trading, it's more insular but ideally you'd learn how to take risk in a controlled manner. 

If your goal is to be a trader at a hedge fund, then learning how to be a disciplined risk taker is what's most important, so I would generally go the prop trader route. 

But it's not always that cut and dry; most people don't have choice A and choice B presented to them simultaneously. It depends on your particular situation. 

Also, if you're prop trading at a small shop, it can't be too small or the experience is not worth much.
